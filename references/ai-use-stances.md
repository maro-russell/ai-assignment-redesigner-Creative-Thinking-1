# AI Use Stances for Course Assignments

This reference describes four intentional stances faculty can take toward AI use in assignments. Each stance serves different learning goals and maps differently onto the TILT framework and AAC&U VALUE rubrics.

---

## Choosing a Stance

The right stance depends on:
1. **What learning goal the assignment targets** — What should students be able to do that AI can't do for them?
2. **Where students are in their learning** — Novices need more scaffolding; advanced students can handle more ambiguity.
3. **The nature of the work** — Creative synthesis assignments call for different stances than skill-building or knowledge-acquisition ones.
4. **Course-level AI policy** — The assignment stance should fit within the broader course or program policy.

**Key principle:** The stance should be *intentional* and *explained to students* — not a default or a hedge. Unexplained AI policies create anxiety and inequity.

---

## The Four Stances

---

### Stance 1: EXPLORATORY
**"Try it, track it, reflect on it."**

Students are encouraged to experiment with AI tools throughout the assignment. The goal is to build metacognitive awareness of what AI does well, what it does poorly, and what it reveals about the student's own thinking.

**Best for:**
- Introductory courses where students are new to a domain
- Assignments explicitly about AI literacy
- Creative Thinking rubric: *Acquiring Competencies* (Model → Adapt level), *Taking Risks*

**What students do:**
- Use any AI tools they choose at any stage
- Keep a detailed AI Use Log
- Reflect substantively on the experience (what AI helped with, what it missed, what surprised them)

**Sample policy language:**
> "You are encouraged to use AI tools throughout this assignment. Think of AI as a thinking partner — something to push back against, build on, or learn from. You must document every meaningful AI interaction in your AI Use Log and write a 150–200 word reflection on what using AI revealed about the task and your own thinking."

**What to assess:**
- Quality and honesty of AI Use Log
- Depth of reflection (not just "AI was helpful/not helpful" — but *how* and *why*)
- Evidence that the student engaged critically with AI output rather than accepting it wholesale

---

### Stance 2: COLLABORATIVE
**"Use AI as a tool at specific stages — and document how."**

AI is integrated as a deliberate partner at named stages of the assignment. The faculty member specifies *where* AI may be used and *for what purpose*, so that AI assists with lower-order work and frees students to focus on higher-order creative or analytical thinking.

**Best for:**
- Assignments with distinct phases (brainstorming → drafting → revising)
- Creative Thinking rubric: *Connecting/Synthesizing/Transforming*, *Innovative Thinking*
- Upper-division courses where students have domain knowledge to evaluate AI outputs

**What students do:**
- Use AI at specified steps (e.g., brainstorming, generating alternatives, proofreading)
- Prohibit or discourage AI at other steps (e.g., the synthesis, the argument, the evaluation)
- Document AI use in an AI Use Log
- Produce a final product that reflects their own synthesis and judgment

**Sample policy language:**
> "AI tools may be used for the following steps: [list steps]. AI tools should NOT be used for [list steps] — these are the places where your thinking and judgment are being assessed. For permitted uses, document your interactions in the AI Use Log. Your final submission must represent your own synthesis and analysis."

**What to assess:**
- Whether the student followed the stated AI permissions
- Quality of the student's own synthesis (the part AI didn't do)
- AI Use Log completeness and honesty
- Evidence that the student pushed beyond AI-generated content

---

### Stance 3: CRITICAL
**"Use AI — then interrogate it."**

Students use AI to generate a response or product, then critically analyze, challenge, or improve upon it using their own disciplinary knowledge. The goal is to develop evaluative judgment by working *against* AI output.

**Best for:**
- Assignments targeting analytical and evaluative thinking
- Creative Thinking rubric: *Embracing Contradictions*, *Solving Problems*, *Innovative Thinking*
- Disciplines where students need to learn quality standards through contrast

**What students do:**
- Prompt an AI to complete the assignment (or a version of it)
- Analyze the AI output: What did it get right? What did it miss? What assumptions did it make? What would a domain expert say is wrong or incomplete?
- Produce their own improved version, with explicit comparison to the AI output
- Reflect on what their critique reveals about the standards of the field

**Sample policy language:**
> "For this assignment, you will first prompt an AI tool to [complete the task]. Then you will write a critical analysis of what the AI produced — identifying its strengths, limitations, and what it reveals about [topic/field]. Finally, produce your own version that addresses the gaps you identified. Submit the AI output, your analysis, and your final product together."

**What to assess:**
- Depth and specificity of the critical analysis (vague critiques = shallow disciplinary knowledge)
- Quality of the student's own improved product
- Evidence of domain knowledge informing the critique
- Sophistication of reflection on what the comparison reveals

---

### Stance 4: RESTRICTED WITH DISCLOSURE
**"AI is limited here — and here's why. If you use it anyway, tell me."**

Faculty restrict AI use for pedagogical reasons and explain those reasons to students. Students are still required to disclose any AI use. This stance avoids blanket prohibition while making the reasoning transparent.

**Best for:**
- Foundational skills assignments where AI use would short-circuit essential learning
- Assessments where individual competency needs to be demonstrated
- Creative Thinking rubric: *Acquiring Competencies* (Model / Adapt levels)
- Any situation where the process of struggle IS the learning

**What students do:**
- Complete the assignment without AI assistance (or with minimal, specified exceptions)
- Disclose any AI use, even if it was minor
- Understand and accept the pedagogical reason for the restriction

**Sample policy language:**
> "Please complete this assignment without AI assistance. Here's why: [specific pedagogical reason — e.g., 'The point of this exercise is to practice generating your own [X] before you can meaningfully evaluate someone else's. AI assistance at this stage would deprive you of that practice.']. If you do use AI in any way — even for brainstorming — please disclose it in a brief note at the end of your submission. Undisclosed AI use will be treated as an academic integrity violation."

**What to assess:**
- Evidence of student's own process and thinking
- Honesty and specificity of any disclosures
- Whether the restriction was justified and clearly communicated (faculty self-check)

## AI Use Log Template

Include this in any assignment where AI use is permitted or required.

```markdown
## AI Use Log
*(Submit this with your assignment)*

| Step in Assignment | AI Tool Used | Prompt I Gave (or summary) | Output Received (summary) | How I Used, Changed, or Rejected It |
|-------------------|-------------|---------------------------|--------------------------|-------------------------------------|
|                   |             |                           |                          |                                     |
|                   |             |                           |                          |                                     |

**Reflection (2–4 sentences):**  
What did AI help you do in this assignment? What did you have to do yourself — and why couldn't AI do that part for you?
```

## Connecting AI Stances to the Creative Thinking Rubric

| Rubric Dimension | Most Aligned AI Stance | Why |
|-----------------|----------------------|-----|
| Acquiring Competencies | Exploratory or Restricted | Students need to practice domain skills before they can evaluate AI's domain performance |
| Taking Risks | Exploratory or Collaborative | AI can lower the stakes for trying something new — or model risk-taking that students then exceed |
| Solving Problems | Collaborative or Critical | AI can generate alternatives; students evaluate and choose |
| Embracing Contradictions | Critical | Students critique AI output to surface hidden assumptions and divergent perspectives |
| Innovative Thinking | Collaborative or Exploratory | AI as a starting point the student pushes beyond |
| Connecting/Synthesizing/Transforming | Any — but synthesis must be student's own | AI can surface connections; the transformation must come from the student |
