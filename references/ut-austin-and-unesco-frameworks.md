# UT Austin & UNESCO AI Frameworks Reference

Two complementary frameworks for grounding assignment-level AI use in institutional and global principles.

---

## PART 1: UT Austin Framework for Responsible Adoption of AI in Teaching and Learning

**Source:** Office of the Executive Vice President and Provost, UT Austin  
**URL:** https://provost.utexas.edu/the-office/academic-affairs/office-of-academic-technology/responsible-adoption-of-ai-tools/

### Definition
Responsible use of AI in teaching and learning is defined as **the adoption of AI that facilitates the achievement of learning outcomes and fosters human development** for all members of the campus community.

AI adoption should be intentional, empowering all students and educators to take full agency over their teaching and learning experiences, strengthen relationships within learning communities, uphold personal integrity and navigate ambiguity. To use AI responsibly, students and educators must always be in the loop, understand how these technologies work, their ethical assumptions and implications, and the influence they will have on their learning, careers and society.

---

### The 8 Principles

**1. Literacy**
Use AI with fluency in its working principles, applications, limitations, and impacts in higher education and beyond, while continuously exploring new AI capabilities and emerging tools.

*Assignment design signal:* Present when students are asked to understand how an AI tool works, evaluate its outputs critically, or reflect on its capabilities and limitations in a domain context.

---

**2. Ethics**
Use AI in ways that recognize the array of ethical implications of technology use on education, the environment, labor, society and other considerations; engage with those implications and consequences across contexts; and consistently practice evaluating when AI can versus should be used.

*Assignment design signal:* Present when students evaluate whether AI use was appropriate, consider broader impacts of AI outputs, or reflect on what was ethically at stake in their choices.

---

**3. Intention**
Use AI transparently and in support of the achievement of learning in and across disciplinary contexts, including knowing when and why it should and should not be used, and embracing the opportunity to design meaningful learning experiences and authentic assessments that help students gain disciplinary knowledge and skills.

*Assignment design signal:* Present whenever the assignment names why AI is (or isn't) being used and connects that choice to a specific learning goal. This principle is almost always active in TILT-designed assignments.

---

**4. Balance**
Use AI with an innovation and future readiness mindset, and with constant awareness of the evolving benefits and drawbacks in the context of teaching and learning across areas of study.

*Assignment design signal:* Present when students are asked to weigh AI's usefulness against its limitations, or when the assignment asks them to consider what AI does well vs. what requires human judgment.

---

**5. Agency**
Use AI in ways that maintain and enhance the agency of humans over their intellectual output, decision-making, and the teaching and learning process.

*Assignment design signal:* Present when students must make meaningful choices that override, redirect, or go beyond what AI produced — and especially when those choices are documented and explained.

---

**6. Relationships**
Use AI in ways that enhance and extend the connections between everyone within a learning community — students and their peers, faculty and students, staff and the people they serve — rather than diminish or replace them.

*Assignment design signal:* Present when assignments involve peer feedback, class sharing, collaborative AI use, or instructor-student dialogue about AI experiences. Also present when the assignment explicitly asks students to discuss AI use with others.

---

**7. Academic Integrity**
Use AI in alignment with the honor code and fundamental scholarly values such as honesty, respect, and authenticity, taking ownership and claiming authorship of the output of tools when appropriate.

*Assignment design signal:* Present whenever an AI Use Log is required, whenever disclosure norms are stated, or whenever the assignment distinguishes between the student's own thinking and AI-generated content.

---

**8. Stewardship**
Use AI in ways that are explicit about how data is being shared, and guardrails are established to uphold the privacy, safety, security, accessibility, intellectual property rights, and right to access of everyone in the teaching and learning community.

*Assignment design signal:* Present when students are prompted to consider what data they are feeding into AI tools, whose work or likeness they are using as inputs, and what happens to that data.

---

### Quick Selection Guide: Which Principles Are Most Active?

| Assignment AI Stance | Most Likely Active Principles |
|---------------------|------------------------------|
| Exploratory | Literacy, Ethics, Agency, Academic Integrity |
| Collaborative | Intention, Balance, Agency, Academic Integrity |
| Critical | Ethics, Literacy, Balance, Intention |
| Restricted with Disclosure | Intention, Agency, Academic Integrity, Ethics |

## PART 2: UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students

**Source:** UNESCO (2024). *AI Competency Framework for Students*. Paris: UNESCO.  
**ISBN:** 978-92-3-100709-5  
**License:** CC BY-SA 3.0 IGO

### Overview
The framework defines 12 competency blocks across two dimensions:
- **4 Aspects** (columns): Human-centred mindset | Ethics of AI | AI Techniques and Applications | AI System Design
- **3 Progression Levels** (rows): Understand → Apply → Create

---

### The 12 Competency Blocks

#### ASPECT 1: Human-Centred Mindset

**1.1 Human Agency** *(Level: Understand)*
Students recognize that AI is human-led and that AI creators' decisions shape how AI impacts human rights and their own lives. They understand what it means for AI to be human-controlled and the consequences when it is not.

*Assignment signal:* When students document where they overrode AI outputs, or reflect on who made decisions at each stage of the work.

---

**1.2 Human Accountability** *(Level: Apply)*
Students recognize that human accountability is a legal and social responsibility when using AI to assist decisions. They understand that humans should not cede determination to AI in high-stakes decisions.

*Assignment signal:* When students must defend their creative or intellectual choices as their own, or when the assignment asks why certain decisions cannot be delegated to AI.

---

**1.3 Citizenship in the AI Era** *(Level: Create)*
Students critically understand the impact of AI on human societies and promote responsible, inclusive design and use of AI for sustainable development. They develop a desire for lifelong learning about AI.

*Assignment signal:* When students are asked to consider broader social implications of AI in their domain, or to advocate for particular uses or limits on AI.

---

#### ASPECT 2: Ethics of AI

**2.1 Embodied Ethics** *(Level: Understand)*
Students develop a basic understanding of ethical issues around AI — including its impact on human rights, social justice, inclusion, equity, and climate — and internalize core ethical principles: do no harm, proportionality, non-discrimination, sustainability, human determination, and transparency.

*Assignment signal:* When students reflect on whether AI use was appropriate, fair, or potentially harmful in the context of the assignment.

---

**2.2 Safe and Responsible Use** *(Level: Apply)*
Students carry out responsible AI practices in compliance with ethical principles and applicable regulations. They protect their data and are aware of typical AI risks and incidents.

*Assignment signal:* When an AI Use Log is required, when data privacy considerations are named, or when the assignment clarifies what information should and should not be entered into AI tools.

---

**2.3 Ethics by Design** *(Level: Create)*
Students adopt an ethics-by-design approach to the design, assessment, and use of AI tools, and to the review and adaptation of AI regulations.

*Assignment signal:* When students evaluate AI tools for ethical compliance, or propose modifications to how AI should be used in a given context.

---

#### ASPECT 3: AI Techniques and Applications

**3.1 AI Foundations** *(Level: Understand)*
Students develop basic knowledge and skills on AI — particularly data and algorithms — and connect conceptual AI knowledge to their activities in daily life.

*Assignment signal:* When students are expected to explain or demonstrate basic understanding of how an AI tool works (e.g., what kind of training data or model underlies it).

---

**3.2 Application Skills** *(Level: Apply)*
Students construct age-appropriate understanding of data, AI algorithms, and programming, and acquire transferable application skills. They critically evaluate and leverage open-source AI tools, libraries, and datasets.

*Assignment signal:* When students use AI tools purposefully for a task, evaluate multiple tools, or make informed choices between AI options.

---

**3.3 Creating AI Tools** *(Level: Create)*
Students deepen and apply knowledge and skills on data and algorithms to customize existing AI toolkits to create task-based AI tools.

*Assignment signal:* When students build, fine-tune, or meaningfully customize an AI tool to address a specific problem — not just use an existing one.

---

#### ASPECT 4: AI System Design

**4.1 Problem Scoping** *(Level: Understand)*
Students understand the importance of AI problem scoping as the starting point for AI innovation. They examine whether AI should be used in a given situation from legal, ethical, and logical perspectives.

*Assignment signal:* When students must justify why AI is or isn't the right tool for a given task, or define the boundaries of what AI can and cannot solve.

---

**4.2 Architecture Design** *(Level: Apply)*
Students cultivate methodological knowledge and technical skills to configure a scalable, maintainable architecture for an AI system.

*Assignment signal:* When students design or configure an AI pipeline, choose among architectural options, or prototype a system.

---

**4.3 Iteration and Feedback** *(Level: Create)*
Students evaluate the appropriateness and robustness of an AI model and its impact on users, societies, and the environment. They make decisions about optimization, reconfiguration, or shutdown.

*Assignment signal:* When students test, refine, or critically evaluate an AI system based on real-world feedback and ethical criteria.
