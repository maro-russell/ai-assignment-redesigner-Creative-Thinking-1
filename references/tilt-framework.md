## What is TILT?

The **Transparency in Learning and Teaching (TILT)** framework structures assignments around three explicit elements that are often left implicit — and that research shows disproportionately disadvantage first-generation, underrepresented, and non-traditional students when unstated.

Making these elements explicit:
- Increases student confidence, sense of belonging, and persistence
- Reduces inequitable gaps in performance
- Improves the efficiency of grading and feedback for faculty
- Makes the pedagogical rationale of assignments legible to students, peers, and administrators

## The Three Elements

### 1. PURPOSE
**Why does this assignment exist?**

Purpose has two components:
- **Skills**: What transferable skills will students practice? (Name them explicitly.)
- **Knowledge**: What disciplinary content will students engage with?

**Key principle:** Purpose should connect to students' lives *beyond* this course. Answer the question students are often afraid to ask: *"Why does this matter?"*

**Examples of weak vs. strong purpose statements:**

❌ Weak: "This paper will help you practice academic writing."

✅ Strong: "This assignment helps you practice synthesizing competing perspectives — a skill used in policy analysis, legal writing, research, and any field where you must make decisions with incomplete information. You'll also build knowledge of [topic] that will ground your work in the rest of this course."

**For AI-integrated assignments, PURPOSE must also address:**
- Why AI is (or isn't) part of this assignment
- What learning goal AI use serves
- What the student — not AI — is responsible for

---

### 2. TASK
**Exactly what should students do?**

Tasks should:
- Use **active verbs** (analyze, draft, revise, compare, document, reflect)
- Be **sequenced** — list steps in the order students should do them
- Specify **format, length, medium** (don't leave these to inference)
- Name **checkpoints** or intermediate deliverables when relevant
- Identify **common mistakes to avoid**
- Be explicit about **where AI may or may not be used at each step**

**Key principle:** Students should not have to decode what to do. Every implicit expectation is a potential equity gap.

**Example of weak vs. strong task statement:**

❌ Weak: "Write a research paper on a topic of your choice related to the course."

✅ Strong:
1. Choose a problem or question from the list provided (or propose your own with instructor approval by Week 3).
2. Identify at least 5 scholarly sources using the library databases. You may use AI to help generate an initial search strategy, but you must locate and read the actual sources yourself.
3. Draft a 1-page outline showing your argument and how your sources support it. Submit by [date].
4. Write a 1500–2000 word paper in which you…
   - Introduce the problem and why it matters (200–300 words)
   - Analyze at least 3 competing perspectives (600–800 words)
   - Synthesize your own position with supporting evidence (400–600 words)
5. Attach your AI Use Log (if applicable) and a 100-word reflection on your process.

---

### 3. CRITERIA
**What does success look like?**

Criteria should:
- Define **characteristics of the finished product** (not just "quality")
- Use **multiple examples** of what success looks like (reduces copying and encourages variety)
- Distinguish **excellent from adequate** in observable, concrete terms
- Connect to the **rubric or learning outcome** being assessed (by name)
- Give students tools to **self-assess** while working

**Key principle:** Don't wait until grading to reveal what you valued. Criteria stated in advance are not a "cheat sheet" — they are equitable instruction.

**Example of weak vs. strong criteria:**

❌ Weak: "Papers will be graded on argument quality, evidence use, and writing."

✅ Strong:
"Your paper will be assessed using the AAC&U Creative Thinking rubric, specifically the dimensions of **Connecting/Synthesizing/Transforming** and **Embracing Contradictions**. Strong work will:
- Bring together perspectives that don't naturally agree and show how they change each other (Synthesis)
- Engage seriously with the strongest counterargument to your position (Embracing Contradictions)
- Go beyond summarizing sources to offer your own interpretation of what they mean together

Adequate work summarizes multiple sources clearly. Strong work shows what those sources mean *when read against each other*."

## TILT + AI: Special Considerations

When an assignment involves AI tools, transparency requires addressing three additional questions in each TILT element:

| TILT Element | AI-Specific Transparency Question |
|-------------|----------------------------------|
| **Purpose** | Why is AI use included (or excluded) — and what learning does that choice serve? |
| **Task** | At which specific steps may/must/may not students use AI? What must they produce themselves? |
| **Criteria** | How will the student's own thinking be distinguished from AI-assisted work in evaluation? |

**The AI Use Log** is the practical tool that makes Task and Criteria transparent when AI is involved. It documents:
- Which AI tools were used
- What prompts were given
- What outputs were received
- How the student used, changed, or rejected those outputs

## Quick Checklist for Transparent Assignment Design

**PURPOSE**
- [ ] Names the specific skills students will practice
- [ ] Names the knowledge students will build
- [ ] Connects to life beyond this assignment/course
- [ ] Addresses the role (or intentional absence) of AI

**TASK**
- [ ] Uses active verbs and sequential steps
- [ ] Specifies format, length, and medium
- [ ] Names common mistakes to avoid
- [ ] Clarifies where/how AI may be used at each step
- [ ] Includes an AI Use Log if AI is permitted

**CRITERIA**
- [ ] Names the rubric or outcome being assessed
- [ ] Translates rubric language into observable student behaviors
- [ ] Distinguishes excellent from adequate in concrete terms
- [ ] Gives students tools to self-assess
- [ ] Addresses how student thinking is evaluated separately from AI contributions
